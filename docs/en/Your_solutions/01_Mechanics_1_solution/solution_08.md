Even though you feel like you are standing still, you are actually spinning around the Earth's axis at over $1,000 \text{ mph}$. Because you are moving in a circle, you are technically accelerating toward the center of the Earth.

---

## The Solution

To find the **Centripetal Acceleration ($a_c$)**, we need to know how fast the Earth is spinning and its size.

### 1. The Given Data

* **Radius ($R$):** $6378 \text{ km} = 6,378,000 \text{ meters}$.
* **Time for one rotation ($T$):** $24 \text{ hours}$.
* To get this in seconds: $24 \times 60 \times 60 = 86,400 \text{ s}$.



### 2. Find the Angular Velocity ($\omega$)

$\omega$ (the Greek letter "omega") represents how many radians the Earth turns per second. A full circle is $2\pi$ radians.


$$\omega = \frac{2\pi}{T} = \frac{2 \times 3.14159}{86,400} \approx 7.272 \times 10^{-5} \text{ rad/s}$$

### 3. Calculate Centripetal Acceleration ($a_c$)

The formula for acceleration in a circle is:


$$a_c = \omega^2 \cdot R$$

$$a_c = (7.272 \times 10^{-5})^2 \cdot 6,378,000$$

$$a_c \approx \mathbf{0.0337 \text{ m/s}^2}$$

---

## Why don't we feel this?

Gravity pulls you down at roughly $9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$. The "centrifugal" effect pushing you out is only $0.034 \text{ m/s}^2$. This means you actually weigh about **0.3% less** at the equator than at the North Pole, but it's such a small difference that your body can't detect it!

---

## Explanation of Letters

* **$a_c$**: Centripetal acceleration (the "center-seeking" acceleration).
* **$R$**: The radius of the circular path (distance from the center of the Earth).
* **$T$**: The Period (the time it takes to complete one full 360-degree rotation).
* **$\omega$ (Omega)**: Angular velocity (how fast the angle is changing).
* **$\pi$ (Pi)**: $3.14159...$ used to relate the radius to the circumference of the circle.
