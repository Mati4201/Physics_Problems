Here are the step-by-step mathematical solutions to your pendulum problems.

### Part 1: Period on the Moon

The formula for the period of a simple pendulum relies on string length ($L$) and gravity ($g$):
$$T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$

We know the period on Earth is **4 seconds**:
$$T_{Earth} = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g_{Earth}}} = 4$$

On the Moon, gravity ($g_{Moon}$) is $\frac{1}{6}$ of Earth's gravity. Substituting this into the formula gives us:
$$T_{Moon} = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{\frac{g_{Earth}}{6}}}$$

By factoring out the $6$ from the denominator of the fraction, we get:
$$T_{Moon} = \sqrt{6} \cdot \left( 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g_{Earth}}} \right)$$

Since the entire expression inside the parentheses is exactly the Earth period (which we know is $4$), we simply multiply:
$$T_{Moon} = 4 \cdot \sqrt{6} \approx 9.8 \text{ seconds}$$

**Answer:** The pendulum's period on the Moon would be approximately **9.8 seconds**.

### Part 2: Length for a 1-Second Period on Earth

To find the required length, we use the exact same period formula but solve algebraically for length ($L$). We will assume standard Earth gravity ($g \approx 9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$):

$$T = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{g}}$$

Substitute the known values ($T = 1$ and $g = 9.8$):
$$1 = 2\pi \sqrt{\frac{L}{9.8}}$$

Now, isolate $L$. First, divide both sides by $2\pi$:
$$\frac{1}{2\pi} = \sqrt{\frac{L}{9.8}}$$

Square both sides to remove the square root:
$$\frac{1}{4\pi^2} = \frac{L}{9.8}$$

Multiply both sides by $9.8$:
$$L = \frac{9.8}{4\pi^2}$$

$$L \approx 0.248 \text{ m}$$

**Answer:** The required length of the pendulum string is approximately **0.248 meters** (or **24.8 cm**).
